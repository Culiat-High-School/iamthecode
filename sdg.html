The Problem

The Digital Divide in Education
Millions of learners worldwide still lack access to computers, tablets, and reliable internet, creating barriers to education and digital literacy. This digital divide is most evident in low-income, rural, and disadvantaged communities, limiting participation in online learning. As education becomes increasingly digital, closing this gap is essential to achieving equitable learning opportunities and the goals of SDG 4.

Out‑of‑School Children and Youth
Despite decades of progress, hundreds of millions of children and adolescents remain out of school. The UNESCO Global Education Monitoring Report notes that around 251 million young people worldwide are not enrolled, with the highest rates in low-income and conflict-affected regions. This ongoing exclusion underscores the persistent barriers to achieving universal quality education under SDG 4.

Girls and Gender Inequality in Education
Gender inequality remains a major barrier to quality education worldwide. Nearly two-thirds of illiterate adults are women, reflecting persistent disparities in access to learning. Socio-economic challenges, cultural norms, and limited opportunities often prevent girls from enrolling or completing school, while higher dropout rates due to early marriage, household duties, and unsafe learning environments further widen the gender gap.

Learning Crisis Among Students
UN and UNESCO data show that around 617 million children and adolescents worldwide do not reach minimum proficiency in reading and mathematics, including 387 million primary school-age children and 230 million lower-secondary adolescents. Most of these students attend school, highlighting that enrollment alone does not ensure learning. Tackling this learning crisis is essential for improving education quality globally.
